People have been asking me to write this book for years.

Life happened — a car accident, losing a friend, buying a mountain home, mentoring adult kids. But the real reason I waited? I knew what this book would cost me personally. And I had to be ready.

It started in a wheelchair. Six years fighting an autoimmune condition so severe I couldn't drive myself home most days. Couldn't carry a five-minute conversation without retreating to my room afterward. Doctors told me to accept this was my life now.

Instead, I enrolled in a Master's degree.

During my capstone, something happened that I still don't fully have language for. I was a year and a half in, wrestling with a hundred-page research paper, pushing ideas around the way I used to push tax code around when I was filing 950 returns in two and a half months.

And my brain lit up.

Everything that had been in a fog from the illness suddenly organized itself. Disciplines that used to live in separate rooms — theology, anthropology, psychology, business, photography, farming — now had connections running between them. I could sit across from someone and see not just the person, but the pain behind the person. The family system. The coping mechanism. The one small shift that could change everything.

A friend told me I was seeing in ten dimensions. I don't know about that. But I know whatever happened during that capstone rewired something permanent.

What started as survival became a system.

I'm now writing REFRAME — hidden mental models for navigating problems and problem people with smarter self-awareness. Every framework was built from real life: a bathroom stall, a get well card, a porcupine, a crop field. Tested on real people in live events across Ecuador, India, Kenya, and North America.

When I'm not writing, I'm farming.

I’m the Managing Partner and CFO of Prairie Land Farms — a commercial mixed grain operation in Southern Alberta, Canada. We grow wheat, canola, barley, peas, and lentils on 3,000 acres.

Our crops end up on tables from Italy to India to Dubai. In 2024, our farm produced enough food to feed over 28,000 Canadian adults for a year.

The farm taught me patience, seasons, and the long game. The book is the same kind of work — plant, nurture, harvest.
